Why reconstruction procurement reform matters more than the money itself

The $524B reconstruction estimate commands headlines. But how Ukraine manages procurement — and whether it can close the gap between committed funds and ground-level project delivery — will determine whether any of it reaches communities.

Reed Research DeskReed Intelligence Group
Published20 April 2026
Read time5 min

The headline number — $524 billion — has become a shorthand for Ukraine's reconstruction challenge. It appears in European Commission damage assessments, World Bank situation reports, and diplomatic communiqués. It is a large and legitimately significant figure. And it has almost nothing to do with the real problem.

The real problem is procurement. Specifically: whether Ukraine can build the institutional capacity to take money that has been committed, convert it into contracts, manage those contracts without diversion, and deliver physical infrastructure at a standard that satisfies both domestic communities and international donor monitoring requirements.

The ProZorro test

Ukraine has one of the most transparent public procurement systems in the world. ProZorro — the electronic procurement platform — was recognized by Transparency International as a global best practice before the full-scale invasion. Every contract above a low threshold is publicly searchable. Every tender is open. Every award is logged.

But ProZorro transparency at the contracting stage does not guarantee delivery. The gap between contract award and physical project completion — where supervision is weaker and verification harder — is where reconstruction money is most vulnerable. The World Bank's 14-prior-action framework for its Rapid Recovery DPF program is implicitly designed to close this gap by requiring Ukraine to install independent oversight mechanisms at the project implementation stage, not just at the procurement stage.

Capacity is the constraint, not capital

The three largest IFI programs in Ukraine — IMF, World Bank, and EBRD — each face the same absorption bottleneck: Ukraine does not currently have enough qualified project implementation capacity to deploy committed funds at the rate donors intend. This is not a corruption problem in the narrow sense. It is an institutional capacity problem. The reconstruction challenge is, at its core, a public administration challenge dressed up in large numbers.
